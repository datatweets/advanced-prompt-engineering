## Lesson Purpose

This lesson teaches you to create social media content using structured messaging strategy rather than vague captions generated on demand. The difference between content that is scrolled past and content that earns attention is almost never about the platform or the format — it is about whether the message was planned before it was written.

You will learn a four-element framework that works across every channel, and how to adapt it for different audiences and platforms without losing the core message.

## The Bait, Hook, Reward, Payload Framework

Most social content fails because it asks for attention before earning it. This framework flips the order.

| Element | Purpose | What it is not |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Payload** | The one message or action you want the reader to carry away | A list of things you want to say |
| **Bait** | The opening line that stops the scroll by creating curiosity or tension | Clickbait — it must be honest and relevant |
| **Hook** | The reason the reader keeps reading — what is at stake for them | A repeat of the bait |
| **Reward** | Genuinely useful information that the reader receives before any ask | A buried pitch or soft promotion |

> **Key insight:** The payload is defined first but delivered last. You need to know what you want the reader to do before you write a single word — but you do not lead with the ask. You earn the right to the ask by delivering a reward.

Think of it this way: a newspaper editorial delivers the most important information first. A social post does the opposite. The opening has to earn the reader's investment, and the reward justifies it. The ask — sign up, read more, share — comes only after the reader has received something useful.

---

## Planning Before Writing

The most common mistake in social content is starting with the draft. Before writing anything, answer these four questions:

1. **What is the single most important thing I want the reader to do or believe after reading this?** (This is your payload.)
2. **What tension, problem, or surprising truth will stop the reader from scrolling past?** (This is your bait idea.)
3. **What is the specific reason this matters to this particular reader right now?** (This is your hook.)
4. **What genuinely useful information can I give them before asking for anything?** (This is your reward.)

If you cannot answer all four, you are not ready to write. The AI tool cannot answer them for you — it can only help you express the answers you already have.

## Scenario: AI Productivity Course Promotion

A company is promoting a one-day AI productivity course aimed at business owners, managers, corporate employees, and students.

The content must:
- Sound practical and credible.
- Avoid promotional language that damages trust.
- Avoid unsupported outcome claims.
- Work across a professional network, email, and internal communications.
- Use no personal names in examples.

**Planning table before writing:**

| Element | Draft for this scenario |
| --- | --- |
| Payload | Register for a practical AI workflow course — or use these ideas immediately |
| Bait | Most teams don't have an AI problem. They have a workflow problem |
| Hook | Meeting notes, email drafts, research summaries — these are where time actually disappears |
| Reward | A five-part prompt structure that turns AI from a chat box into a work assistant |

Once this table is complete, writing the post takes a few minutes. Without it, writing the post takes much longer and usually produces something generic.

## Planning Prompt

```text
Act as a content strategist for social media.

We need a post about this offer:
[describe the offer in two to three sentences]

Target audience:
[role, situation, level of familiarity with the topic]

Channel:
[professional network / email newsletter / internal announcement]

First, define the four message elements:
1. Payload — the one thing we want the reader to remember or do
2. Bait — the opening that stops the scroll (no clickbait — must be honest)
3. Hook — why this matters to this specific reader right now
4. Reward — the useful idea or information the reader receives before any ask

Then write a draft post for this channel using these elements.

Rules:
- No emoji unless the brand guide requires them.
- No hashtags unless requested.
- No personal names.
- No outcome guarantees or unsupported statistics.
- Plain, direct English.
- The reward must be genuinely useful — not a restatement of the offer.
```

## Channel Adaptation Prompt

The core message must stay consistent across channels. The tone, length, and structure change.

```text
Adapt this post for three channels:
1. Professional network (LinkedIn-style: direct, credible, professional)
2. Email newsletter teaser (longer, more context, conversational)
3. Internal company announcement (clear, factual, no hype)

For each version, return:
- Channel name
- Audience in this context
- Main angle for this channel
- Draft post
- Call to action specific to this channel

Rules:
- Keep the same payload across all versions.
- Do not copy-paste the same text.
- Each version should feel natural on its channel.
```

## The Five Opening Lines Test

The opening line is the most important line in a social post. It determines whether anyone reads the rest. Run this prompt to generate and compare options:

```text
Write five different opening lines for a post about [topic].

Use these five approaches, one each:
1. A direct statement of a problem
2. A surprising or counterintuitive claim (that is honest and supportable)
3. A question that creates immediate relevance
4. A number that is specific and credible
5. A contrast (most people do X, but the teams that succeed do Y)

For each opening, add one sentence that would follow naturally.

Then recommend which opening is strongest and explain why.
```

Read all five before choosing. The opening you would have written instinctively is rarely the strongest one on the list.

## Example: Full Post (Professional Network)

```text
Most teams don't have an AI problem. They have a workflow problem.

The actual time loss isn't in finding the tool. It's in meeting notes that never get summarized, 
customer emails that sit in drafts, research that stays scattered across bookmarks, 
and weekly reports that take longer to write than the work itself.

A practical prompt has five parts: task, context, constraints, format, and review criteria.
That structure turns AI from a blank chat box into something worth having.

Our one-day workshop teaches this workflow through real business scenarios and practice exercises 
— including a section on responsible review before anything leaves your desk.
```

What makes this work:
- Bait in the first line creates immediate tension.
- Hook in the second paragraph makes it personal and specific.
- Reward in the third paragraph gives the five-part structure — genuinely useful before any ask.
- Payload in the final paragraph is the offer, delivered after the reader already has something.

## Review Prompt

```text
Review these posts.

For each post, check:
1. Is the payload clear — would a reader know what to do or remember?
2. Does the bait earn attention honestly, without misleading?
3. Is the reward genuinely useful before the ask appears?
4. Are there any unsupported claims or outcome guarantees?
5. Does the tone fit the channel?
6. Is there a clear, specific call to action?
7. Would this text feel out of place on this platform?

Return a table with:
Post | Element | Issue | Severity | Fix
```

## Avoiding Common Social Content Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Leading with the offer.**
"Join our workshop on AI productivity" is a poster, not a post. It starts with what you want, not with something the reader cares about.

**Mistake 2: Outcome claims without evidence.**
"Cut your admin time in half" requires evidence. If you do not have it, remove the claim.

**Mistake 3: Identical posts across channels.**
A professional network post is not an email. An internal announcement is not a promotional post. Each channel has a different relationship with its reader.

**Mistake 4: Confusing reward with promotion.**
A reward is information the reader can use immediately — right now, in their next hour of work. "Learn more at our workshop" is not a reward. "Here is the five-part prompt structure" is.

**Mistake 5: Treating opening lines as interchangeable.**
"Here is our latest update" and "Most teams don't have an AI problem" are both opening lines. Only one earns the next scroll.
