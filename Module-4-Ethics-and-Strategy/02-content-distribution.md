## Lesson Purpose

This lesson teaches you how to plan and execute content distribution using AI as a planning tool. You will learn how to connect the content you create in Module 3 to the right audience, at the right stage of their decision journey, through the right channel — on a schedule a small team can actually maintain.

Distribution is where most content efforts fail. Great articles get written and forgotten. Social posts go out without a clear purpose. The same message gets sent to every channel in the same format. This lesson fixes that.

## Start With One Goal

Distribution planning fails when it tries to accomplish everything at once. Before building any calendar, choose one primary goal:

| Goal | What you are trying to do | Success looks like |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Awareness | Reach new people who have never heard of you | Views, reach, first-time visitors |
| Education | Help an existing audience understand something new | Time on page, saves, downloads, replies |
| Lead generation | Encourage people to make contact or register | Form submissions, inquiries, sign-ups |
| Trust building | Reduce hesitation for people considering a decision | FAQ clicks, demo requests, direct replies |
| Retention | Keep existing customers or students engaged | Repeat use, template adoption, feedback |

A campaign can have secondary goals, but everything in the plan should serve the primary one. If a content piece does not clearly serve the goal, it should not be in the plan.

## The Audience Journey

Every person in your audience is at a different stage. Content that works for someone in awareness mode will not work for someone ready to decide — and content designed for decision mode will feel premature to someone who has just discovered the topic.

| Stage | The audience question | Best content type |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Awareness | What is this problem and why should I care? | Short post, explainer, checklist, introductory article |
| Consideration | What are my options and what should I look for? | Comparison guide, FAQ, workshop, video walkthrough |
| Decision | Why should I trust this specifically? | Case example, demo, risk review, implementation plan |
| Onboarding | How do I actually start? | Step-by-step tutorial, prompt template, quick-start guide |
| Retention | How do I keep getting value? | Advanced workflow, updated prompt library, review process |

> **Key insight:** Most content is created for awareness and consideration, because those stages feel like marketing. Onboarding and retention content is often more valuable — because it works with people who have already committed. It is also the content that generates the most useful evidence for future market research.

## Scenario: One Article, Four Audiences, Two Weeks

A training provider has published a practical blog post: "How to Use AI for Weekly Project Updates."

The audiences are:
- Business owners (decision-focused: will this save time and reduce risk?)
- Department managers (workflow-focused: how does this fit our process?)
- Corporate employees (practical and cautious: is this safe to use at work?)
- Students (career-focused: will this skill be expected in the workplace?)

The team is small. Two weeks of distribution. One piece of core content. Four audiences. The challenge is to adapt the message to each audience and channel without creating entirely new content for each.

## Distribution Prompt

```text
Act as a content distribution strategist.

Core content:
[describe the article or asset being distributed]

Primary campaign goal:
[awareness / education / lead generation / trust / retention]

Audience segments:
[list each segment and their primary concern]

Available channels:
[list channels — professional network, email, internal comms, events, etc.]

Team constraints:
[describe capacity — one person, two weeks, etc.]

Return a distribution calendar table with these columns:
Day | Channel | Audience segment | Journey stage | Message angle | 
Format | Owner role | Review step | Success metric

Rules:
- Keep the schedule realistic for the stated team capacity.
- Adapt each message angle to the specific audience and stage.
- Include a review step for all public-facing items.
- Do not copy the same message across channels — adapt.
- Avoid unsupported outcome claims.
```

## Adapting One Piece of Content

This is one of the highest-value uses of AI in a content workflow. You have done the hard work of creating something useful. AI can help you extract multiple angles from it — without each version sounding like a copy of the original.

```text
Act as a content adaptation specialist.

Source content:
[paste the original article, post, or summary]

Produce three adapted versions:

Version 1 — Professional network post (awareness stage):
Audience: [segment]
Goal: Stop the scroll and create curiosity. No hard sell.
Length: 150–250 words.
Tone: Direct, credible, no hype.

Version 2 — Email teaser (consideration stage):
Audience: [segment]
Goal: Give enough value to earn a click through to the full article.
Length: 100–150 words.
Tone: Conversational, clear subject line, one call to action.

Version 3 — Internal team announcement (onboarding stage):
Audience: [segment — employees, students, or team members]
Goal: Encourage immediate use of the workflow described.
Length: 80–120 words.
Tone: Practical, factual, no promotion.

Rules for all versions:
- Same core message, different angle.
- No personal names.
- No unsupported claims.
- Each version must feel natural for its channel.
```

## Cadence: What a Small Team Can Realistically Do

| Team capacity | Realistic two-week cadence |
| --- | --- |
| One person, part-time | Two strong posts + one email + one internal note |
| One person, dedicated | One article + three posts + one email + one internal |
| Two-person team | Full two-week calendar: article, email sequence, posts by segment, one event |
| Larger team | Add segmentation, A/B testing, and performance reviews per channel |

> **Tip:** It is better to publish three pieces consistently every two weeks than to publish ten pieces once and then disappear. Consistency builds the trust and audience expectation that makes each new piece more likely to be read.

## Measurement: Matching Metrics to Goals

| Campaign goal | What to measure | What it tells you |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Awareness | Views, reach, new visitors | Are we reaching the right new people? |
| Education | Time on page, saves, downloads | Is the content useful enough to keep? |
| Lead generation | Form submissions, sign-ups | Are we converting interest into contact? |
| Trust building | FAQ clicks, demo requests, direct replies | Are we reducing hesitation? |
| Retention | Repeat visits, prompt library adoption | Are existing customers getting ongoing value? |

Choose one primary metric per content piece. If you cannot name a single metric that would tell you whether the piece worked, you do not have a clear enough goal yet.

## Review Before Publishing

Every public content item needs a review step before it goes out. This is especially important when AI has helped with the drafting.

```text
Review this [post / email / announcement] before publishing.

Check:
1. Is every claim in this piece supported by evidence we actually have?
2. Does the message fit the stage — awareness, consideration, decision, or retention?
3. Does the call to action match the goal of this specific piece?
4. Is the tone appropriate for this channel and audience?
5. Is any customer, employee, or partner name included that should not be?
6. Are there any outcome promises that require verification?

Return a table with:
Issue | Risk level | Fix
```

## Practice

Take one piece of content from Module 3 (blog post, social content, or storyboard).

Create:

1. A two-week distribution calendar with at least six items.
2. At least three audience-specific message angles from the same source content.
3. One primary metric for each item.
4. A review note for every public-facing item.
5. A capacity assessment: what is realistic for you with the team and time you have?

---

## Review Checklist

- [ ] Is the campaign goal specific — not "grow our audience" but "generate 20 course sign-ups in two weeks"?
- [ ] Does each content item serve the stated goal?
- [ ] Is each message angle adapted to the audience and journey stage?
- [ ] Are public-facing items reviewed before publishing?
- [ ] Is the cadence realistic for your team's actual capacity?
- [ ] Does every item have a clear, measurable metric?
